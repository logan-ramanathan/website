Hydrogen collisional-radiative model

Quantifying the sensitivity of Balmer-line spectroscopy for hydrogen plasma diagnostics using a collisional-radiative model.

PythonApril 2026Brown University

Sensitivity maps of the Hβ/Hα and Hγ/Hβ line ratios across the (Tₑ, nₑ) parameter space.

Overview

Measuring electron temperature and density is essential across fusion research, spacecraft propulsion, and industrial plasmas. Balmer-line spectroscopy offers a non-invasive way to do it — but only in the regimes where line ratios actually depend on the plasma parameters. I built a collisional-radiative (CR) model of atomic hydrogen to map exactly where those Balmer diagnostics are reliable, and where they break down.

The model tracks 15 energy levels, using electron-impact rate coefficients from Vriens & Smeets (1980) and oscillator strengths from Johnson (1972), with Einstein A-coefficients verified against tabulated quantum-mechanical values to better than 0.1%.

Approach

  • Assembled the full rate matrix — electron-impact excitation/de-excitation, ionization, spontaneous radiative decay, and three-body recombination — and solved the steady-state populations with linear algebra (numpy.linalg.solve).
  • Validated against the published results of Vinoth et al. (2022), and independently against the analytic coronal (low-density) and Saha–Boltzmann / LTE (high-density) limits.
  • Extended the model to a time-dependent mode, integrating the rate equations forward with a stiff BDF solver (scipy.integrate.solve_ivp) to test oscillating plasma conditions.
  • Used eigenvalue analysis of the rate matrix to extract the system's natural equilibration timescales independently of the full integration.

Key findings

  • Temperature sensitivity of the Balmer ratios is confined to kTₑ ≲ 5 eV.
  • Density sensitivity peaks in the collisional-radiative transition region, nₑ ∼ 1011–1013 cm−3.
  • For oscillating conditions, the steady-state diagnostic assumption breaks down near ∼100 kHz at nₑ = 1010 cm−3, and only near ∼1 MHz at nₑ = 1015 cm−3 — and the time-dependent response shows nonlinear amplification effects.

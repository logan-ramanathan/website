Self-compensating Langmuir probe

Building a plasma diagnostic from scratch to characterize an RF discharge for space-propulsion research.

Stanford, CASummer 2026w/ Prof. Cappelli & Dr. Biswas

Overview

At the Stanford Plasma Physics Lab, I'm designing and building a self-compensating Langmuir probe from scratch to measure electron density, temperature, and electron energy distribution functions (EEDFs) across an RF inductively-coupled plasma (ICP) discharge — work aimed at plasma diagnostics for space-propulsion applications.

Approach

  • Designing the probe with tunable RF chokes for self-compensation and an actuated probe tip so measurements can be taken across a range of spatial locations and drive frequencies.
  • Building an optical emission spectroscopy workflow to infer plasma properties from line-ratios via an argon collisional-radiative model.
  • Performing VNA-based microwave interferometry to measure line-integrated electron density as an independent cross-check.

Why it matters

Cross-validating three independent diagnostics — probe, spectroscopy, and interferometry — builds confidence in the measured plasma parameters and helps characterize discharges relevant to electric propulsion.
